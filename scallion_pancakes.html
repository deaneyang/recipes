Scallion Pancakes

All amounts below are rough estimates. Adjust as needed.

Ingredients
  • Approximately 2 cups Flour
  • Approximately 1 cup Water
  • 1/4 cup of scallions, chopped finely
  • Salt
  • 2 tablespoons of vegetable oil (but not olive oil)

Ingredients for dipping sauce (optional)
  • 3 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon vinegar
  • Ginger root, chopped finely
  • (optional) Hot sauce, Chinese chili sauce or siracha
  • (optional) Fresh cilantro, chopped finely
  • Whatever else you think would taste good

Tools needed
  • Rolling pin
  • Pastry brush
  • Large flat surface generously coated with flour
  • Skillet

Instructions
  1. First, make the dough. This is the most difficult step, because I don't know the exact amounts or proportions of flour and water. The goal is to make a dough that is similar in texture to bread dough but drier. The trick is to learn by trial and error. Do the rest of the steps and see how good the results are and adjust accordingly. Also, check to see whether there is too much or too little dough and adjust for the next time. In any case, you do roughtly the following:
    1. Put the flour in a large mixing bowl.
    2. Add the water and mix first with a wooden spoon and then your hands.
    3. Add water or dough according to whether the dough is too wet and sticky or too dry. Don't be afraid to switch if you've overadjusted one way or another.
    4. Once the dough has the right texture, form into small balls about the size of a golf ball. Coat generously with flour.
  2. Generously coat a flat dry surface with flour.
  3. Roll one of the balls into a flat disk. It needn't be perfectly circular. The goal is to roll it as thin as possible.
    1. If the dough is too wet or elastic, this won't work. This can be solved by just adding more flour to the flat surface as you roll the dough until its texture is still somewhat elastic but dry.
    2. If the dough has the right consistency, you will be able to apply a lot of pressure, almost all of your body weight, as you roll.
    3. Rotate the dough between rolls (or between every 2-3 rolls), so that it maintains a roughly circular shape.
    4. Flip the dough occasionally, so both sides are similar in moisture or dryness.
    5. If a hole appears, try to pinch it shut and continue rolling. If a few small holes persist, just continue.
    6. Ideally, you want the dough to be thin enough that you can see faintly the surface below. But if this is too hard to do the first few times, just continue with whatever you can do.
  4. Adding the remaining ingredients:
    1. Put the oil in a small bowl.
    2. Brush the top of the now thin dough generously with oil.
    3. Sprinkle some salt on the dough (on the surface of the oil).
    4. Sprinkle some but not too much chopped scallions on the the dough. Again, you'll figure out how much via trial and error.
    5. Now comes another tricky part. You want to slowly roll the dough as tightly as possible into a long thin cylinder with many layers of the dough with layers of oil, scallions, salt in between.
  5. Creating the layers of the pancake:
    1. Now, keeping the dough on the table, shape it back into a disk by forming a tight (horizontal) spiral disk. Mush it gently with your hand to flatten it a bit and tighten the spiral. As you reach the end of the cylinder, tuck the last bit under the spiral disk.
    2. Using the rolling pin, roll the disk formed by the spiral into a not-too-thin disk, say about a 1/4 inch thick. As you get better, you can make this thinner (but not too thin) to get a more flaky result.
    3. If you didn't make the spiral tight enough, it will separate as you roll it. Just reform the spiral and try again.
  6. Cooking the pancakes:
    1. Heat the skillet to medium-high heat (yes, you'll adjust this as you make the pancakes).
    2. Add enough oil to coat the entire bottom with a not-too-thin layer.
    3. Fry the pancake on each side until both sides have nicely browned spots, where the pancake touches the pan, and feel or sound crispy.
  7. Eating the pancakes
    1. They should be eaten immediately.
    2. Slice into quarters or eighths and eat. The pieces might fall apart due to the spiral but that's OK.
    3. If you want, prepare the dipping sauce by mixing the ingredients in a small bowl.
    4. Adjust the proportion of soy sauce and vinegar according to taste.
    5. But if the amount of scallions and salt is just right and the pancake is nice and flaky, then it's delicious as is.
  8. Making more:
    1. After you make one, you can adjust both the ingredients and the steps to improve the results. You'll also find that just by making more, the results improve, even if you didn't consciously change anything.
    2. Once you get good enough results, you can make several uncooked pancakes, coat each generously with flour, and stack them
    3. After you've made enough uncooked ones, you can fry them all up and serve each one as they are cooked.
