Potstickers

All amounts below are rough estimates. Adjust as needed.

Ingredients for dough
  • Approximately 2 cups Flour
  • Approximately 1 cup Water
Ingredients for dipping sauce (optional)
  • 3 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon vinegar (we use white)
  • Ginger root, chopped finely
  • (optional) Hot sauce, Chinese chili sauce or siracha
  • (optional) Fresh cilantro, chopped finely
  • Whatever else you think would taste good
Ingredients for filling

Our filling is not for purists. We add tofu to it, making it lighter than the usual filling. We find it just as tasty, but you can eat way more potstickers this way.

  • 2 lbs ground pork
  • Fresh green vegetables, chopped as finely as possible>
    • Amount should be roughly the same or twice the volume as the pork
    • Possibilities
      • Best: Jiu cai (韭菜), also known as Chinese chives or garlic chives
      • Mustard greens
      • Some other leafy green vegetable, ideally something with some bitterness or strong flavor
      • Last resort: Thawed out frozen spinach, with water squeezed out (this is what my parents used)
  • 14 oz soft tofu
  • Chunk of ginger root, chopped coarsely
  • 3 cloves garlic, chopped coarsely
  • Several tablespoons soy sauce
  • Salt, to taste
  • 1/2 teaspoon sesame oil
  • Whatever else you think would taste good
Ingredients for cooking
  • Large flat skillet (preferably NOT nonstick)
  • Lid for skillet
  • Vegetable oil (We use high heat safflower or sunflower oil. Do NOT use olive or other oils with strong flavors that will clash with the dumplings)
  • White vinegar
  • Water
Tools needed
  • Rolling pin
  • Large flat surface generously coated with flour
  • Skillet
Instructions
Dough

This is the most difficult step, because I don't know the exact amounts or proportions of flour and water. The goal is to make a dough that is similar in texture to bread dough but drier. The trick is to learn by trial and error. Do the rest of the steps and see how good the results are and adjust accordingly. Also, check to see whether there is too much or too little dough and adjust for the next time. In any case, you do roughly the following:

  1. Put the flour in a large mixing bowl.
  2. Add the water and mix first with a wooden spoon and then your hands.
  3. Add water or dough according to whether the dough is too wet and sticky or too dry. Don't be afraid to switch if you've overadjusted one way or another.
  4. Err on the side of being a little too moist, because it's easy to add more flour when you roll out the skins.
  5. Once the dough has the right texture, form into fist-sized balls. Roll with your hands into long cylinders, about an inch in diameter. Coat generously with flour.

Filling
  1. Put about a quarter of the tofu, all of the garlic, ginger, soy sauce, sesame oil, and whatever else you wantn ingredients into a food processor. Pur\'ee.
  2. Pour mixture, pork, and vegetables into a large bowl and mix by hand.
  3. Taste it (yes, it's raw pork but a little bit won't do any harm). Add more soy sauce, sesame oil, or salt, according to your taste. It can be left a bit undersalted, since the dipping sauce will add more saltiness.
Skins
  1. Generously coat a flat dry surface and the rolling pin with flour.
  2. Cut each cylinder of dough into 1 inch pieces, and reshape each piece into a ball.
  3. Roll each ball into a flat disk. Rotate the disk between 1 or 2 rolls, to keep the shape as close to a circle as you can. As it flattens, put more and more weight onto the rolling pin. The goal is to make a thin round disk. We try to make it as thin as possible but without it tearing easily. This requires the dough to have just the right elasticity. It does not have to be a perfect circle.
  4. If the skin starts to fall apart because it's too dry. You'll need to make the rest of the dough by reforming all of it into a single ball, drizzle some water on it, and kneading it a bit. Or consider tossing the dough and start again.
  5. If the skin sticks to the rolling pin, then it's too moist. Just add more flour as you roll the skins.
  6. The traditional method is to make the center thicker than the ediges. You can see how Chinese usually do this in this video. This results in thicker skins and a heavier potsticker. We prefer thinner skins, because it makes the potsticker lighter.

Cooking the potstickers
  1. In a small glass, put half water and white vinegar.
  2. Add a generous layer of cooking oil (NOT olive or any other flavored oil) to the skillet and heat to medium high or high heat.
  3. Arrange dumplings in skillet in concentric circles and one or two in the cetner.
  4. Pour vinegar-water mixture into pan (watch out for splattering!) and immediately cover with lid. Keep heat on high.
  5. Listen carefully. When the liquid has boiled off, you'll start to hear some crackling. Let it cook for another minute or two. Lift lid and slice into one of the dumplings. If it's not fully cooked, add a little more water and cover. When they're cooked, use a metal spatula to carefully scrape dumplings of the skillet bottom and place onto a large plate. Serve.
Eating the potstickers
  1. Mix dipping sauce ingredients.
  2. Carefully dip each potstickers into the sauce and eat. They will be hot and slippery.
Making more:
  1. Host a party and train your guests to make the potstickers.
  2. Keep encouraging your guests, so they really get into it.
  3. Cook some periodically for the guests, so they can taste how good the potstickers are.
  4. You'll end up with way more potstickers than the guests can eat.
  5. Place remaining uncooked dumplings on generously cookie sheets and put them into the freezer for about 30-60 minutes (longer is OK).
  6. You now have lots of premade dumplings that can be cooked and eaten any time. Just follow the exact cooking instructions above. Do not thaw them first.
